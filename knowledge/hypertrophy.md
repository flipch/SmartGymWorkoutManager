# Hypertrophy Training Knowledge (Evidence-Based)

A curated, citation-backed reference for a resistance-training program generator. The goal
is muscle hypertrophy. Numbers are drawn from peer-reviewed meta-analyses and the
Renaissance Periodization (Israetel) volume-landmark framework. Where a figure is debated,
approximate, or extrapolated, it is flagged as such.

> **Caveat up front.** Most "optimal" numbers below are population averages or practitioner
> heuristics, not precise individual prescriptions. Volume landmarks in particular are
> *individual ranges to be calibrated through experience*, not universal constants. The
> engine should treat them as **starting points** and adjust based on recovery, soreness,
> and performance feedback.

---

## 1. Weekly Volume Landmarks (sets per muscle per week)

Volume is the strongest-supported driver of hypertrophy. Schoenfeld, Ogborn & Krieger
(2017) meta-analysis found a **graded dose-response**: ~+0.37% muscle gain per additional
weekly set, with 10+ sets/week superior to 5-9, which beat <5. More recent and larger
meta-analyses (e.g., the 2025 Springer dose-response meta-regression) continue to show
benefit accruing well beyond 10 sets for many individuals, with diminishing returns at the
top end and rising injury/fatigue cost.

**Counting rule:** a "set" = a hard working set taken reasonably close to failure
(roughly 0-4 RIR; see §3). Sets far from failure should be discounted. Compound lifts
distribute stimulus across multiple muscles (e.g., a row counts toward back *and* biceps).

### Renaissance Periodization landmark definitions (Israetel/Hoffmann)

- **MV — Maintenance Volume:** minimum to retain existing muscle (~6 sets/week for most
  muscles; can be lower for small muscles getting indirect work).
- **MEV — Minimum Effective Volume:** smallest weekly volume that drives *new* growth.
- **MAV — Maximum Adaptive Volume:** the productive working range where most growth per
  unit of fatigue happens; this is a *range*, and you progress through it across a block.
- **MRV — Maximum Recoverable Volume:** ceiling beyond which fatigue outpaces recovery and
  gains stall. Most variable landmark; shifts with sleep, stress, age, and caloric state.

**Mesocycle logic:** start a block near MEV, add ~1-3 sets/muscle/week as recovery allows,
push toward MRV over ~4-6 weeks, then deload to ~MV before repeating. Staying chronically at
high volume is inferior to cycling MEV → MAV → MRV → deload.

## 2. Intensity / Load: rep ranges, %1RM, and effective load zone

Schoenfeld & Grgic's "repetition continuum" re-examination (2021) and the low- vs high-load
meta-analysis (Schoenfeld et al. 2017) establish that **hypertrophy is similar across a wide
load spectrum when sets are taken close to failure.** Load is not the primary lever for
growth — proximity to failure and volume are.

- **Effective hypertrophy rep range:** ~**5-30 reps** per set, provided sets approach
  failure. The classic "6-12" is *efficient* (good stimulus-to-time and stimulus-to-fatigue),
  but 5-30 all work.
- **%1RM correspondence (approximate):**
  - ~5 reps ≈ 85% 1RM
  - ~8 reps ≈ 80% 1RM
  - ~10 reps ≈ 75% 1RM
  - ~12 reps ≈ 70% 1RM
  - ~15 reps ≈ 65% 1RM
  - ~20 reps ≈ 60% 1RM
  - ~30 reps ≈ ~50% 1RM (lower bound of the effective zone)
  - (Estimates vary by exercise and individual; multi-joint lifts tolerate more reps at a
    given %1RM than isolation lifts.)
- **Practical split:** heavier loads (5-8 reps, 75-85% 1RM) suit big compounds where joint
  stress at high reps is fatiguing; moderate-to-light (10-20 reps, 60-75%) suit isolation
  and machine work — relevant for a machine-based gym. Very low loads (<30% 1RM / >30 reps)
  are *not* recommended for efficient hypertrophy.

## 3. Proximity to Failure (RIR / RPE)

RIR = Repetitions In Reserve (how many more reps you could have done). The RIR-based RPE
scale (Zourdos et al. 2016) maps **RPE 10 = 0 RIR**, RPE 9 = 1 RIR, RPE 8 = 2 RIR, etc.

- Robinson/Refalo et al. (2024) dose-response meta-regression: **hypertrophy increases as
  sets get closer to failure, but the curve flattens** — benefit past ~1-2 RIR is small.
  Going *to* failure adds little growth over ~1-2 RIR while adding disproportionate fatigue
  and recovery cost.
- Strength gains are relatively insensitive to proximity-to-failure; training too close to
  failure can even slightly impair strength via accumulated fatigue.
- **Practical target:** most hypertrophy work at **0-3 RIR**, with the productive sweet spot
  **1-3 RIR**. Reserve true failure (0 RIR) for the last set of an exercise and for safe,
  single-joint/machine movements where failure carries low injury risk.
- **Periodize effort across a block:** start a mesocycle slightly easier (e.g., ~3 RIR) and
  progress toward ~0-1 RIR by the final loading week, then deload.
- RIR accuracy improves with training experience; novices systematically *underestimate*
  effort (stop further from failure than they think) — bias novices slightly closer to
  failure or use the last-set-to-failure heuristic.

## 4. Frequency (sessions per muscle per week)

- Schoenfeld, Ogborn & Krieger (2016) frequency meta-analysis: **2x/week beat 1x/week** for
  hypertrophy. The 2019 update (Schoenfeld, Grgic, Krieger) found **no significant difference
  when weekly volume is equated** — i.e., frequency is largely a *tool to distribute volume*,
  not an independent driver.
- **Implication:** frequency matters mostly because higher frequency lets you accumulate more
  quality weekly volume per muscle without excessive per-session fatigue/junk volume.
- **Practical:** train each muscle **≥2x/week**. Once total weekly volume is high (e.g.,
  >~10-12 hard sets/muscle), split it across 2-4 sessions so no single session exceeds
  ~6-8 hard sets for that muscle (sets beyond that in one session show diminishing per-set
  return). Frequency ≥2 also keeps muscle protein synthesis elevated more of the week.

## 5. Progressive Overload

Overload = progressively increasing the training stimulus over time. Mechanisms, roughly in
order of practicality:

1. **Load** — add weight when reps at a given RIR exceed the target.
2. **Reps** — add reps at a fixed load (the basis of double-progression).
3. **Sets** — add weekly sets across a mesocycle (the MEV→MRV ramp in §1).
4. **Density** — same work in less time (shorter rest) or more work in same time. A minor
   lever for hypertrophy; primarily conditioning/work-capacity.
5. **Tempo / ROM / improved technique** — secondary contributors.

**Double progression (recommended default for the engine):** pick a rep range (e.g., 8-12).
Keep load fixed until you hit the *top* of the range for all prescribed sets at target RIR;
then increase load and drop back to the bottom of the range. Repeat.

- Increment guidance: ~2.5-5 kg (or one machine pin / smallest plate) for compounds; smaller
  jumps (1-2.5 kg) or microloading for isolation/small muscles.
- Progress should be tracked at matched RIR — beating a log only counts if effort is equal.

## 6. Exercise Selection & Muscle Balance

A plan that "trains the wrong things" usually means: too much pressing vs pulling, all
vertical and no horizontal, quad-dominant with neglected posterior chain, or over-training
already-overworked muscles (front delts, upper traps) while neglecting antagonists (rear
delts, mid-back, hamstrings/glutes). Balance protects joints and produces proportional
physiques.

### Movement-pattern coverage (aim to include all)

- **Horizontal push** (bench/chest press, push-up) ↔ **Horizontal pull** (row).
- **Vertical push** (overhead press) ↔ **Vertical pull** (pulldown/pull-up).
- **Knee-dominant** (squat, leg press, leg extension — quads) ↔ **Hip-dominant**
  (hip hinge/RDL, leg curl, hip thrust — hamstrings/glutes).
- **Agonist/antagonist pairs:** chest↔back, biceps↔triceps, quads↔hamstrings,
  front delts↔rear delts.

### Ratio guidance (practitioner heuristics, not from RCTs — flagged as approximate)

- **Push : Pull volume ≈ 1 : 1 to 1 : 2** (bias toward pulling). Modern desk-bound lifters
  and those with cranky shoulders benefit from *more* pulling, often **2 pulls : 1 push**.
- **Horizontal pull : Vertical pull ≈ 2 : 1** — horizontal rows bias rhomboids / mid &
  lower traps (scapular retractors), the muscles most people under-train and that most
  support shoulder health.
- **Rear-delt work** should roughly match or exceed direct front-delt work; front delts are
  already hammered by all horizontal/vertical pressing, so *direct* front-delt work is often
  unnecessary.
- **Hip-dominant : Knee-dominant ≈ at least 1 : 1.** Hamstrings/glutes/posterior chain are
  commonly under-trained relative to quads; ensure dedicated hip-hinge and knee-flexion
  (leg curl) work — squats/leg-press alone under-stimulate hamstrings.
- Reference data (Baker & Newton type analyses) suggest balanced upper-body pressing and
  pulling strength is associated with healthier shoulder mechanics; large pressing
  dominance is a common imbalance to avoid.

### Common imbalances to prevent (engine validation checks)

- Pressing volume > pulling volume.
- All vertical pulling (pulldowns), no horizontal rowing.
- Direct front-delt volume ≥ rear-delt volume.
- Quad volume >> hamstring/glute volume.
- Biceps volume >> triceps (triceps are the larger arm muscle).

## 7. Rest Intervals

- Schoenfeld et al. (2016) RCT: **3-min** rest produced greater hypertrophy and strength than
  **1-min** in trained men over 8 weeks (longer rest preserved per-set volume/load).
- More recent meta-analyses (e.g., the 2024 Bayesian "Give it a rest" review) find rest
  duration has at most a **trivial** effect on hypertrophy once per-set performance is
  maintained — i.e., the real mechanism is *don't let short rest erode your reps/load*.
- **Practical:**
  - **Compound / multi-joint lifts:** ~**2-3 min** (longer for heavy, fatiguing lifts).
  - **Isolation / machine work:** ~**1-2 min** is usually sufficient.
  - Minimum useful guidance: rest long enough to roughly repeat your target reps at the
    target RIR. Sub-60s rest tends to cut strength/volume and is generally not recommended
    for maximizing hypertrophy.

## 8. Tempo / Time-Under-Tension & the Eccentric

Relevant because the target machine supports an **eccentric-overload mode**.

- Schoenfeld, Ogborn & Krieger (2015) tempo meta-analysis: hypertrophy is **similar across
  rep durations of ~0.5-8 s**. **Very slow tempos (>10 s/rep) are inferior.** There is no
  hypertrophy advantage to deliberately super-slow lifting within the normal range.
- The **eccentric (lengthening) phase matters**: eccentric-only training can produce
  **greater or equal** hypertrophy vs concentric-only, and combined eccentric+concentric is
  the practical standard. Eccentrics generate high force at long muscle lengths and notable
  mechanical tension / muscle damage.
- Eccentric hypertrophy appears **velocity-independent when load and total time-under-tension
  are matched** — fast vs slow eccentrics grow similar muscle if TUT/load are equated. So a
  controlled (not collapsed) eccentric is the goal; extreme slowness isn't required.
- **Eccentric-overload mode guidance:** loading the eccentric heavier than the concentric is
  a legitimate way to increase tension/stimulus and can be used as a progression lever or for
  accentuated-eccentric blocks. Because eccentrics cause more muscle damage and soreness,
  **count eccentric-overload sets as higher-fatigue** — bias toward the *lower* end of the
  volume range when programming them, and ensure adequate recovery/frequency spacing.

**Default tempo for the engine:** controlled eccentric ~2-4 s, brief or no pause, concentric
~1-2 s (intent to move fast, actual speed slows near failure). Avoid sets where total rep
duration routinely exceeds ~8-10 s.

Default starting volume for an intermediate: ~MEV + 2-4 sets (low end of MAV).

### Rep ranges by emphasis
```
strength_bias_compound:   5-8 reps   (~80-85% 1RM)
hypertrophy_default:       6-12 reps  (~67-80% 1RM)  <- most efficient zone
hypertrophy_metabolite:    12-20 reps (~60-70% 1RM)
endurance/light:           20-30 reps (~50-60% 1RM)  <- still works if near failure
effective_overall_window:  5-30 reps near failure
```

### RIR targets by phase
```
week_1 (block start):  ~3 RIR
mid_block:             ~2 RIR
peak_loading_week:     ~0-1 RIR (last set may hit failure)
deload:                ~4-5 RIR, ~half volume
sweet_spot_general:    1-3 RIR
novices:               bias slightly closer to failure (they under-rate effort)
```

### Rest intervals
```
heavy_compounds:    120-180 s (2-3 min)
moderate_compounds: 90-150 s
isolation/machines: 60-120 s
hard_minimum:       >= 60 s (sub-60s erodes volume/strength)
```

### Frequency
```
per_muscle_min:        2x / week
cap_per_session:       ~6-8 hard sets per muscle (split surplus across sessions)
rule:                  use frequency to distribute weekly volume, not as a separate goal
```

### Tempo / eccentric
```
rep_duration_effective:  0.5-8 s total (avoid > 10 s)
default_eccentric:       2-4 s controlled
default_concentric:      1-2 s (fast intent)
eccentric_overload:      legitimate progression lever; treat as HIGH fatigue
                         -> program at lower end of volume range, ensure recovery
```

### Muscle-balance ratios (heuristic, approximate)
```
push : pull                = 1:1 to 1:2 (bias to pull)
horizontal_pull : vertical = ~2:1
rear_delt : front_delt     >= 1:1 (often skip direct front-delt work)
hip_dominant : knee_dom    >= 1:1
triceps >= biceps volume   (triceps are the larger arm muscle)
```

### Progressive overload default
```
method:        double progression
rep_window:    e.g. 8-12; raise load when top of window hit at target RIR for all sets
load_increment: 2.5-5 kg compounds / 1-2.5 kg isolation (or one machine pin)
volume_ramp:   +1-3 sets/muscle/week from MEV toward MRV over 4-6 wk, then deload to ~MV
```
